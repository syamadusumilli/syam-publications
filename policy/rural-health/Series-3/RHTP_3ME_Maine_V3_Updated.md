# RHTP 3.ME: Maine

## The Oldest State Confronts Rural Health Crisis

*Series 3: State Implementation*

Maine received **$190 million in FY2026 RHTP funding**, positioning the state to address rural health challenges compounded by the nation's oldest population. The award, developed through robust stakeholder engagement led by the Department of Health and Human Services, focuses on five priorities: empowering rural Mainers toward healthy living, strengthening the workforce, advancing technology adoption, reducing access barriers, and sustaining rural health infrastructure.

The state's five-year projected total of **approximately $950 million** arrives as Maine faces twin crises. Rural hospitals rank among the worst financial performers nationally. Eleven birthing units have closed in the past decade, leaving roughly half of Maine's 36 hospitals without delivery services. RHTP provides historic investment, but federal Medicaid cuts estimated at **$5 billion over ten years** will erode the revenue base RHTP cannot replace.

## Rural Health Landscape

### State Context

Maine is the **second most rural state in the nation**, with over 60% of its 1.4 million residents living in rural areas. The state spans **35,385 square miles** across 16 counties, with population concentrated along the southern coast while vast northern and eastern regions remain sparsely populated. Washington County in Downeast Maine and Aroostook County covering the northern third exemplify frontier conditions where communities are separated by hours of travel.

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total Population | 1.4 million |
| Rural Population | 60%+ (840,000+) |
| Counties | 16 |
| Median Age | 45.1 years (oldest nationally) |
| Population 65+ | 24%+ (highest nationally) |

**Maine holds the distinction of having the oldest population** of any state, with median age exceeding 45 years and nearly one-quarter of residents over 65. This demographic reality shapes every aspect of healthcare delivery: demand for geriatric services grows while workforce replenishment stalls and younger families face fewer reasons to stay.

The number of Mainers over age 65 is projected to **increase 36.2% by 2030** according to the Maine Office of the State Economist, compounding a workforce crisis the state already cannot solve.

### Healthcare Infrastructure

Maine's hospital landscape includes 36 facilities serving a geographically dispersed population:

| Facility Type | Count | Notes |
|---------------|-------|-------|
| Prospective Payment System (PPS) Hospitals | 17 | Major acute care facilities |
| Critical Access Hospitals | 16 | Rural and frontier locations |
| Other Hospitals | 3 | Specialized facilities |
| Rural Health Clinics | 60+ | Primary care access points |
| Federally Qualified Health Centers | 20+ | Safety net providers at 100+ sites |

The **16 Critical Access Hospitals** serve Maine's most rural communities, qualifying for cost-based Medicare reimbursement that provides financial lifeline for low-volume facilities. CAHs experienced 76% decline in charity care costs following Medicaid expansion, demonstrating the direct link between coverage and rural hospital sustainability.

### Financial Distress

Maine's hospitals face structural financial crisis documented in an April 2025 Maine Hospital Association analysis:

| Metric | PPS Hospitals Rank | CAH Hospitals Rank |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Operating Margin | 46th of 50 states | 17th of 50 states |
| Debt Burden | 49th of 50 states | 24th of 50 states |
| Facility Age | 46th of 50 states | 40th of 50 states |

**Approximately half of Maine hospitals operate at loss at any given time.** Steven Michaud, president of the Maine Hospital Association, characterized the situation: "It's not something I like to say, but we're not done with closure of rural OB units."

The financial crisis reflects multiple compounding factors:
- **Oldest population nationally** drives higher utilization costs
- **Large rural geography** requires infrastructure investment exceeding patient revenue
- **Government payer dependence** with reimbursement rates lagging cost increases
- **Workforce shortages** forcing reliance on expensive traveling providers

Hospital traveler costs remain **50% higher than pre-pandemic levels**, representing structural expense increases that ongoing operations cannot absorb.

### Workforce Crisis

Maine hospitals are actively recruiting for **4,227 budgeted positions**, with nurses and certified nurse assistants in greatest demand. The workforce crisis extends across every specialty and setting:

| Shortage Area | Scope |
|---------------|-------|
| Hospital Positions | 4,227 open positions |
| Home Care Workers | 2,300 additional FTEs needed |
| Unfulfilled Home Care Hours | 23,500 weekly |
| Physician Distribution | Top 4 counties: 396.5/100K; Bottom 4: 120.5/100K |

**Workforce aging compounds recruitment challenges.** More than one-third of rural physicians in Maine are aged 60 or older. In Maine's most rural counties, half or more of physicians were age 55 or older, indicating near-term retirement risk. The workforce is aging faster than replacement pipelines can fill.

Geographic disparity in physician distribution reveals the rural penalty: Cumberland, Androscoggin, Kennebec, and Penobscot counties average nearly 400 physicians per 100,000 residents, while Oxford, Sagadahoc, Somerset, and Washington counties average barely 120.

Jess Maurer, executive director of the Maine Council on Aging, summarized the trajectory: "We're not making more people and our workforce shortage is just going to grow and we have more care needs. If Maine doesn't figure it out, the entire health system is heading towards collapse."

### Maternity Care Collapse

**Eleven birthing units have closed in Maine over the past decade**, with four closures in 2024-2025 alone. Roughly half of Maine's 36 hospitals no longer offer birthing services. The closures span the state but have hit rural areas hardest:

Recent and announced closures include:
- Calais Community Hospital (2017)
- York Hospital
- Rumford Hospital (2023)
- Mount Desert Island Hospital (2024)
- Houlton Regional Hospital (2024)
- MaineHealth Waldo Hospital (2025)
- Northern Light Inland Hospital (closing late 2025)

Rural Mainers whose closest hospital lacks birthing services now face **average 45-minute drives** to reach delivery care, the longest in New England. High-risk pregnancies may require travel to Bangor, Portland, or Boston for specialist care.

**Nearly half of all births statewide are covered by MaineCare**, rising to 55% at rural hospitals. This payer mix vulnerability means Medicaid cuts will disproportionately affect remaining rural maternity services.

The pattern reflects unsustainable economics: ten of Maine's 21 remaining birthing hospitals had fewer than 200 births annually in 2023, well below the volume considered viable by national standards.

## RHTP Award and Context

### Funding Analysis

| Metric | Maine | National Context |
|--------|-------|------------------|
| FY2026 Award | $190,008,051 | Average $200M |
| Five-Year Projection | ~$950 million | Assumes consistent allocation |
| Rural Population | 840,000+ | 60%+ of state |
| Per-Capita (Rural) | ~$226 | Upper-middle tier |
| Award Rank | ~24th | Mid-range |

Maine's award reflects the RHTP formula structure:
- **$100 million baseline** from equal state distribution
- **$90 million workload component** based on rurality metrics and application scoring

The state's high rural population percentage and application quality contributed to above-baseline workload allocation despite relatively small absolute rural population.

### Medicaid Context

**MaineCare covers nearly 400,000 Mainers**, representing approximately 29% of state population. Coverage reaches across the demographic spectrum: about two-thirds of nursing home residents, half of children, and nearly half of hospital births.

**Medicaid Expansion Status:** Maine voters approved Medicaid expansion via **ballot measure in November 2017** with 59% support. Implementation began January 2019 under Governor Janet Mills after the previous administration delayed implementation despite voter mandate. **106,587 people** currently enrolled through expansion, including 94,407 adults without children and 12,180 parents and caretaker relatives.

**Medicaid expansion reduced uncompensated care** across Maine hospitals by $126 million during the first two years of the COVID pandemic. Charity care costs declined 65% on average across all hospitals and 76% at Critical Access Hospitals between 2018 and 2021. Research indicates non-expansion states experienced uncompensated care increases during the same period.

### Medicaid Cut Exposure

H.R. 1 provisions create estimated **$5 billion in MaineCare cuts over ten years**, fundamentally reshaping coverage and provider economics:

| Provision | Impact | Timeline |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Eligibility Redeterminations | Every 6 months vs annually | January 2027 |
| Retroactive Coverage | Reduced to 1-2 months | January 2027 |
| Immigrant Coverage Elimination | ~3,000 enrollees lose coverage | October 2026 |
| Family Planning Moratorium | Planned Parenthood and Maine Family Planning | Effective July 2025 |
| Projected Coverage Loss | ~34,000 Mainers | Various |

The Center for American Progress projects **$86 million increase in uncompensated care** by 2034 as Medicaid cuts push tens of thousands off coverage. This reverses the gains achieved through expansion.

**Maine's RHTP award of $190 million annually cannot offset Medicaid losses.** As Maine DHHS explicitly acknowledged, RHTP funding is "likely to be only a fraction of the $5 billion that Maine is estimated to lose under the law."

## State Approach

### Governance Structure

**Maine Department of Health and Human Services** serves as lead agency, with Commissioner Sara GagnÃ©-Holmes directing implementation in collaboration with the Governor's Office of Policy Innovation and the Future. The application development process incorporated extensive stakeholder engagement prioritizing rural community perspectives.

Supporting organizations include:
- **Maine Hospital Association** representing 36 hospitals
- **Maine Primary Care Association** coordinating community health centers
- **Northern Light Health and MaineHealth** as major health systems
- **Consumers for Affordable Health Care** advocating for coverage access
- **Maine State Chamber of Commerce** representing employer perspectives

The application received support from all four members of Maine's Congressional delegation, with Senator Susan Collins noting: "I proposed $50 billion in funding for the Rural Health Transformation Program to help give rural states like Maine the resources they need to keep health care available close to home."

### Five Strategic Priorities

Maine organized its RHTP application around five priorities developed through public engagement:

**Priority 1: Empowering Rural Mainers for Healthy Living**
Supporting evidence-based prevention and chronic disease management aligned with MAHA wellness objectives. Focus on interventions addressing root causes of disease rather than solely treating symptoms.

**Priority 2: Strengthening the Rural Health Workforce**
Addressing the 4,227 open hospital positions through recruitment incentives, training pipeline expansion, and retention strategies. Includes loan repayment programs requiring minimum three-year rural service commitments.

**Priority 3: Adopting Health-Enabling Technologies**
Expanding telehealth capacity and electronic medical record adoption to extend specialty access. Particularly critical for behavioral health given shortage of in-person psychiatric providers.

**Priority 4: Reducing Financial and Transportation Barriers**
Addressing the non-medical factors preventing rural Mainers from accessing care. Transportation challenges in the second-most rural state create barriers even where services exist.

**Priority 5: Sustaining Long-Term Viability**
Building operational models for rural health systems that can survive beyond RHTP funding. Recognizes that temporary federal investment must catalyze permanent transformation.

## Social Care Integration

### SDOH and HRSN Initiatives

Maine's RHTP application addresses social determinants within its barrier reduction priority but does not create standalone SDOH screening infrastructure. The state's approach integrates social care components into broader access initiatives:

**Transportation:** Leveraging existing MaineCare Non-Emergency Transportation (NET) service that enables travel to out-of-state specialists. RHTP may expand transportation support for services not currently covered.

**Financial Barriers:** Charity care expansion through LD 1508 (signed July 2025) simplifies hospital charity care applications and expands eligibility to 400% of federal poverty level. This state action complements RHTP investment.

**Food Security:** Community health center integration with SNAP enrollment assistance. Maine's 176,000+ SNAP recipients face benefit reductions under H.R. 1 provisions.

### Community Health Worker Programs

Maine's RHTP application incorporates CHW development within workforce priorities. The state lacks comprehensive CHW certification infrastructure compared to states with established programs.

**Training Pipelines:** Washington County Community College and University of Maine at Machias offer healthcare career classes that could expand to include CHW certification.

**Deployment Model:** CHWs proposed for home visiting, care navigation, and community health outreach. Particularly relevant given aging population and nursing home closure patterns.

**Sustainability Question:** CHW programs require ongoing employment funding. Hospital or health system employment models face same financial pressures affecting other positions.

### CIE and Referral Infrastructure

Maine does not currently operate statewide Community Information Exchange infrastructure comparable to states like Montana (CONNECT) or Oregon (Connect Oregon).

**Current Landscape:**
- Maine 211 provides information and referral services
- Individual health systems operate referral networks
- Community health centers coordinate with social services locally

**RHTP Opportunity:** Technology investment priority could support CIE procurement, though the application does not specify closed-loop referral platform deployment.

**Vendor Gap:** Maine presents market opportunity for SDOH platforms seeking state-level deployment. The absence of mandated statewide CIE creates procurement pathway.

Maine's social care strategy relies on existing infrastructure (MaineCare NET, Maine 211, FQHC coordination) rather than building new statewide platforms. The barrier reduction priority addresses transportation and financial access but does not create systematic SDOH coordination infrastructure comparable to CIE-focused states.

## Assessment

### Strengths

**Medicaid Expansion Benefits:** Maine's 2019 expansion reduced uncompensated care by $126 million and improved hospital financial stability. Critical Access Hospitals experienced 76% charity care reduction. This baseline protects against worse outcomes even as federal cuts erode gains.

**Stakeholder Alignment:** Broad coalition support spanning hospitals, employers, consumers, and all Congressional delegation members suggests implementation pathway with minimal political obstruction. Governor Mills' consistent rural health focus provides executive leadership continuity.

**Community Health Center Network:** Over 100 service sites across all 16 counties provide primary care infrastructure that RHTP can strengthen. FQHCs maintained access during COVID and expansion periods when hospital systems faced greater disruption.

**Roux Institute Partnership:** Northeastern University's Maine-based research facility provides workforce analysis and solution development capacity. The Institute's maternal health workforce research directly informs strategies for addressing birthing unit closures.

**State Legislative Action:** LD 1508 charity care expansion and healthcare workforce initiatives demonstrate state willingness to complement federal investment with policy reform.

### Vulnerabilities

**Financial Baseline:** With approximately half of hospitals operating at loss and debt burden ranking 49th nationally, transformation investments compete with survival needs. Technology and workforce investments matter little if underlying revenue models remain broken.

**Demographic Trajectory:** The oldest-in-nation population will intensify healthcare demand while workforce ages out faster than replacement occurs. The 36.2% projected increase in residents over 65 by 2030 creates demand surge that current infrastructure cannot absorb.

**Maternity Care Collapse:** Eleven birthing unit closures in a decade signals service-line contraction that RHTP alone cannot reverse. When hospitals face survival choices, low-volume obstetrics becomes elimination target regardless of community need.

**Medicaid Cut Exposure:** The $5 billion estimated MaineCare cuts over ten years dwarf $950 million in RHTP investment. Coverage losses will increase uncompensated care, eroding the financial improvement expansion achieved.

**Traveler Dependency:** The 50% traveler cost premium above pre-pandemic levels represents structural expense increase. Recruitment incentives may reduce traveler reliance gradually, but competitive labor markets make permanent workforce capture uncertain.

### Social Care Survival Prospects

**SDOH Screening:** At risk. No dedicated screening infrastructure investment. Provider-level implementation depends on individual facility capacity and competing priorities.

**HRSN Navigation:** Uncertain. Barrier reduction priority addresses access but does not specify navigation workforce or coordination infrastructure.

**CHW Deployment:** Partial support. Workforce priority includes CHW development but lacks scale comparable to dedicated programs in states like Oregon or Massachusetts.

**CIE Platform:** Gap exists. Maine lacks statewide closed-loop referral infrastructure. RHTP technology investment could address but not specified in application priorities.

**Assessment:** Maine's RHTP application emphasizes medical system transformation and workforce over social care infrastructure. Existing services (Maine 211, MaineCare NET) provide foundation but lack integration that dedicated CIE investment would enable.

### The 2030 Question

Maine's sustainability priority appropriately frames the fundamental challenge: what survives when RHTP ends?

**Scenario Analysis:**

*If Medicaid cuts proceed as enacted:*
- 34,000 Mainers lose coverage
- Uncompensated care increases $86 million annually
- Rural hospital margins worsen despite RHTP investment
- Maternity unit closures continue
- Workforce migration accelerates toward higher-paying urban markets

*If RHTP workforce investments succeed:*
- Recruitment incentives bring providers to rural Maine
- Three-year service commitments provide temporary stability
- Retention depends on practice environment and compensation competitiveness
- Post-commitment departure risk remains

*If technology adoption expands:*
- Telehealth extends specialty access to underserved areas
- EHR interoperability improves care coordination
- Infrastructure investment requires ongoing maintenance funding
- Technology cannot substitute for absent workforce

**The arithmetic problem:** Maine hospitals ranking 49th in debt burden cannot assume debt for transformation investments. State revenue capacity faces competing demands. Federal investment ending in 2030 creates cliff effect for initiatives requiring ongoing support.

## Conclusion

Maine's $190 million RHTP award provides meaningful investment in a rural health system under extraordinary stress. The second-most rural state with the nation's oldest population faces challenges that money alone cannot solve: workforce aging faster than replacement, maternity services collapsing county by county, and hospitals accumulating debt while operating at loss.

The Mills Administration developed a thoughtful application prioritizing workforce, technology, prevention, access barriers, and sustainability. Broad stakeholder support and Congressional backing suggest implementation can proceed without political obstruction. The state's Medicaid expansion experience demonstrates capacity for coverage-driven improvement.

The structural challenges remain formidable. Federal Medicaid cuts estimated at $5 billion over ten years will erode the coverage gains that stabilized hospitals following 2019 expansion. RHTP's $950 million five-year investment cannot offset this loss. When Senator Collins characterized the funding as helping "rural states like Maine," the implicit acknowledgment was that help differs from solution.

Social care integration receives limited dedicated attention in Maine's application. The state lacks CIE infrastructure that would enable systematic SDOH screening and closed-loop referrals. CHW development appears within workforce priorities but not at scale comparable to states with established programs.

For Maine's 840,000 rural residents, many of them aging in communities where hospitals close and maternity care disappears, RHTP represents federal acknowledgment of crisis. **Whether acknowledgment translates to sustainable improvement depends on execution, state investment, and factors beyond any single program's control.**

The Pine Tree State has survived through adaptation for four centuries. Rural health transformation requires adapting to realities that precede RHTP and will persist long after its sunset.
