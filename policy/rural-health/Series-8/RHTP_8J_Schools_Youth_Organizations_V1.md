# RHTP 8.J
# Schools and Youth Organizations
## Future Investment vs. Current Crisis

Rural schools are often the last institutional anchor in communities losing everything else. When the hospital closes, the factory leaves, and the downtown empties, the school gymnasium still hosts basketball games. The elementary school still employs teachers. The building still gathers community members who share little else. **Schools represent both the community's past and its claim on a future.**

Youth organizations extend this function through structured programming: 4-H clubs teaching agricultural science and leadership, mentoring programs connecting young people to adult guidance, sports leagues and summer camps providing structure and supervision. These organizations invest in people who will be adults in twenty years, community members in thirty, healthcare workforce in forty.

RHTP runs through 2030. A health careers program graduating students in 2035 produces no providers during the funding period. A school-based health center serving students today delivers current services but may not survive federal funding withdrawal. **The tension between future investment and current transformation shapes how schools and youth organizations can participate in RHTP, and whether their participation represents transformation or temporary service expansion.**

This article examines schools as community institutions, school-based health centers as healthcare delivery mechanisms, youth organizations as workforce pipeline components, and the fundamental question of whether RHTP's five-year timeline can accommodate investments whose returns arrive after funding ends.

## Schools as Community Institutions

### The Rural School's Unique Position

In rural communities, schools occupy institutional positions that urban schools rarely hold. **The school is often the largest employer**, with teaching and support staff constituting significant portions of local employment. The school building is the largest public gathering space, hosting town meetings, church services when sanctuaries are too small, and election polling. The school schedule structures community rhythm: summer means empty hallways and families on vacation; fall means football games and community gathering.

This institutional centrality creates both opportunity and burden. Schools are expected to serve functions beyond education: social services, healthcare access, community development, economic anchor. Consolidation pressures that have closed thousands of rural schools since 1930 threaten not just educational access but **community identity itself**.

**Approximately 9,000 rural school districts** serve students across the United States, ranging from districts with thousands of students to one-room schools serving handfuls of children across vast geographic areas. Funding varies dramatically: property-wealthy districts with minimal tax effort may outspend property-poor districts with maximum tax effort. State equalization formulas partially address disparities but cannot eliminate them.

### Schools and Health

The connection between education and health is bidirectional and well-documented. **Children who are healthy attend school more consistently** and perform better academically. Children who complete more education have better health outcomes across their lifespans. Educational attainment predicts life expectancy more strongly than income alone.

Schools affect health through multiple mechanisms:

**Direct health services:** School nurses, vision and hearing screenings, immunization requirements, and in some cases school-based health centers provide healthcare access within school settings.

**Health education:** Curriculum covering nutrition, physical activity, substance use, sexual health, and mental health affects health knowledge and potentially health behaviors.

**Physical environment:** School meals, physical education requirements, recess policies, and building environments affect student health directly.

**Social environment:** Bullying, peer relationships, adult mentorship, and school climate affect mental health and wellbeing.

**Future opportunity:** Educational achievement creates pathways to employment, income, and resources that support lifelong health.

Rural schools often struggle to provide these functions. **School nurse ratios in rural areas frequently exceed recommended maximums**, with some schools sharing nurses across multiple buildings or lacking nursing staff entirely. Health education curriculum may be limited by teacher comfort, community values, or resource constraints. Physical education and recess may be squeezed by academic accountability pressures.

## School-Based Health Centers

### What School-Based Health Centers Provide

School-based health centers (SBHCs) represent the most intensive model of school-health integration. **SBHCs are full-service healthcare facilities located within or adjacent to school buildings**, providing primary care, behavioral health services, and often dental and vision care to enrolled students. Unlike school nursing, which provides limited services, SBHCs function as healthcare providers with billing relationships, medical records, and comprehensive service capacity.

The SBHC model has grown substantially over the past two decades. **From 1,135 SBHCs in 1998-99, the number grew to approximately 3,900 by 2021-22.** Growth accelerated after the Affordable Care Act provided $200 million for SBHC expansion in underserved communities. The model has extended from its urban origins into rural and suburban settings.

Key SBHC characteristics:

**Comprehensive services:** Most SBHCs provide primary care by definition. 65% provide behavioral health services. 41% have expanded care teams including oral health, vision, nutrition, or other specialties.

**Multiple delivery models:** 90% use traditional school-based facilities. Others use mobile models, school-linked clinics at nearby facilities, or telehealth connections.

**Varied sponsorship:** FQHCs sponsor the largest share of SBHCs, followed by hospitals and health systems, school districts, health departments, and nonprofit organizations.

**Funding diversity:** SBHCs rely on Medicaid and CHIP billing, state grants, federal grants, private foundation support, and school district contributions. No single funding stream supports most centers.

### Rural SBHCs

**Approximately 35% of SBHCs serve rural communities**, a share that has grown as the model expanded beyond urban origins. Rural SBHCs face distinct challenges:

**Staffing:** Health professional shortages affect SBHC staffing just as they affect other rural healthcare settings. Recruiting nurse practitioners, behavioral health providers, and support staff to rural school settings is difficult.

**Sustainability:** Rural SBHCs often serve smaller student populations, limiting billing revenue. Medicaid reimbursement provides essential support, but many students lack insurance or have private insurance with limited SBHC coverage.

**Technology infrastructure:** Telehealth-enhanced SBHCs can extend specialist access to rural students, but broadband limitations constrain telehealth effectiveness in many rural areas.

**Community values:** SBHCs in conservative rural communities may face resistance to certain services, particularly reproductive health. State laws and local policies limit SBHC scope in some settings.

### Evidence on SBHC Effectiveness

Research on SBHC effectiveness, while methodologically limited, suggests positive effects:

**Healthcare access and utilization:** SBHCs increase receipt of preventive services, particularly for students from low-income families and students of color. Vaccination rates, well-child visits, and routine care increase with SBHC access.

**Educational outcomes:** Studies associate SBHC access with improved attendance, reduced chronic absenteeism, and in some cases improved academic performance. A 2025 study of rural New York SBHCs found students with SBHC access had significantly fewer absences than comparison students. The study examined 66,303 students across 52 schools, finding that **differences were greatest at lower levels of absenteeism and among elementary students**, suggesting SBHCs help prevent attendance problems before they become chronic.

**Health outcomes:** Evidence suggests SBHCs improve asthma management, reduce emergency department utilization, and support behavioral health. Students with asthma using SBHC services show better medication adherence and fewer acute episodes. Emergency department visits for conditions manageable in primary care settings decline when students have SBHC access. Effects on chronic disease management and long-term health outcomes are less well-documented.

**Behavioral health services:** The growing emphasis on behavioral health in SBHCs addresses a critical gap in rural youth services. **65% of SBHCs provide behavioral health services**, making schools one of the most accessible settings for youth mental health care in communities lacking child psychiatrists and psychologists. With youth mental health declining nationally, school-based behavioral health access becomes increasingly valuable.

**Equity effects:** Because SBHCs disproportionately serve low-income students and students of color, positive effects contribute to reducing health disparities. SBHCs can increase access to preventive and routine care for students from families with low incomes and those who are racially or ethnically minoritized. In elementary schools, SBHCs can increase use of preventive care services for young children of color receiving Medicaid.

However, **research evidence on rural SBHCs specifically is limited**. The Community Preventive Services Task Force notes that most SBHC studies evaluate urban settings, and questions remain about effectiveness in low-density areas where different SBHC models may be necessary. The Task Force specifically identifies "How effective are SBHCs in rural areas with low population density in which a different design of SBHCs may be necessary?" as an evidence gap requiring additional research.

### SBHC Financing and Sustainability

Understanding SBHC financing illuminates sustainability challenges:

**Medicaid and CHIP billing** provides the primary revenue stream for most SBHCs. Students enrolled in Medicaid or CHIP can receive SBHC services billed to those programs. However, billing covers only portion of SBHC operating costs, and significant portions of students lack billable coverage (uninsured or privately insured with plans that do not cover SBHC services).

**State grant programs** provide essential supplementary funding. States like Oregon, New York, Michigan, and Colorado have dedicated SBHC grant programs that support operations beyond what billing revenue provides. **In 2024, Michigan released $4.46 million and New York released $20 million** in state grants to expand SBHCs. States without such programs see SBHCs struggling for sustainability.

**Federal grants** through various HRSA programs, ACA investments, and other federal sources support SBHC operations and expansion. However, grant funding is time-limited, creating the sustainability challenge RHTP would replicate.

**Sponsoring organization resources** sometimes subsidize SBHC operations. Hospital-sponsored SBHCs may receive institutional support as community benefit. FQHC-sponsored SBHCs can allocate 330 grant resources to school-based sites. District-sponsored SBHCs may receive school budget allocations.

**The sustainability question for RHTP** is whether expansion funded through RHTP grants can transition to sustainable financing when grants end. SBHCs in states with strong state support, high Medicaid enrollment, and committed sponsoring organizations have sustainability pathways. SBHCs in states without these conditions may close when RHTP funding ends, leaving communities with service expectations they cannot meet.

## Youth Organizations

### The 4-H Model

**4-H is the nation's largest youth development organization**, with over six million members nationwide. Operating through the land-grant university cooperative extension system, 4-H provides structured programming in agriculture, STEM, healthy living, and civic engagement. The organization's rural roots remain evident: 4-H serves rural communities more intensively than most national youth organizations.

4-H's structure connects federal investment (through USDA's National Institute of Food and Agriculture), state leadership (through land-grant universities), and local implementation (through county extension staff and volunteers). This structure provides institutional stability that many youth organizations lack.

**Health-related 4-H programming** includes:

- Healthy living curricula covering nutrition, physical activity, and mental health
- Agricultural safety education for youth working on farms
- STEM programming that can connect to healthcare career exploration
- Leadership development that builds civic capacity relevant to health advocacy

4-H's rural reach makes it potentially valuable for workforce pipeline development. Youth exposed to health careers through 4-H programming in middle school might pursue healthcare training in their twenties and return to rural practice in their thirties. **The timeline exceeds RHTP's horizon**, but the capacity exists.

### Other Youth Organizations

Beyond 4-H, rural communities host various youth organizations with potential health connections:

**FFA (Future Farmers of America):** Agricultural education organization with 850,000+ members. Provides leadership development and career exploration, including agricultural health and safety. FFA's structure through secondary schools and career and technical education programs positions it as a potential healthcare workforce pipeline component. Agricultural health connections (veterinary science, food safety, occupational health) could extend to human health careers.

**Youth sports leagues:** Provide physical activity and adult mentorship. May connect to health through coaching relationships and organized activity. **In communities where school sports represent primary organized youth activity**, coaching relationships create mentorship opportunities that can guide career exploration including healthcare.

**Faith-based youth programs:** Church youth groups, Vacation Bible School, and similar programs reach rural youth through trusted institutions. As Article 8.A documents, faith-based organizations often constitute the primary community infrastructure in rural areas. Their youth programs reach young people who may not participate in school-based activities, extending potential workforce pipeline reach.

**Mentoring programs:** Big Brothers Big Sisters and similar programs provide adult mentorship that can affect health through relationship quality and guidance. Research associates mentoring relationships with improved educational outcomes, reduced risk behaviors, and increased career aspirations. **Connecting mentoring programs to healthcare career exploration** could extend workforce pipeline functions.

**Summer programs:** Camps, enrichment programs, and summer employment provide structured activities during school breaks. Healthcare-focused summer programs (hospital volunteering, health careers camps, medical simulation experiences) expose youth to healthcare environments during intensive periods that school-year programming cannot provide.

**AmeriCorps and service programs:** Programs like Michigan State University Extension's 4-H HealthCorps place AmeriCorps members in communities to deliver mental health and wellness programming. These programs provide **both current service delivery and workforce development**, as AmeriCorps members often pursue healthcare careers following service.

These organizations generally lack direct healthcare connections. Their health relevance operates through **indirect pathways**: physical activity, mentorship, skill development, and community connection that support health behaviors and wellbeing. Converting this diffuse health relevance into structured healthcare workforce pipeline contributions requires intentional program design and healthcare partner engagement that most youth organizations currently lack.

### Health Careers Pipeline Programs

Several models attempt to convert youth organization presence into explicit healthcare workforce development:

**Health Occupations Students of America (HOSA):** National organization for secondary and postsecondary students pursuing healthcare careers. HOSA chapters exist in career and technical education programs across the country, providing competitive events, leadership development, and career exploration. **Rural HOSA chapters** connect students to healthcare careers but face limitations when local healthcare employers are distant or nonexistent.

**Area Health Education Centers (AHECs):** The AHEC network, funded through HRSA, provides health careers pipeline programming in many states. AHEC programs offer summer camps, shadowing experiences, and mentorship connecting rural youth to healthcare careers. Where AHECs have strong rural presence, they represent established pipeline infrastructure that RHTP could strengthen.

**Hospital-sponsored programs:** Health systems in some regions sponsor health careers programming for local youth. These programs range from summer camps to multi-year academy models with curriculum, clinical exposure, and scholarship support. Hospital sponsorship provides sustainability that grant-funded programs lack, but depends on hospital commitment to workforce development investment.

**Community college partnerships:** Articulation agreements between high schools and community colleges can accelerate healthcare career entry. Students completing health-related coursework in high school may earn college credit, reducing time to credential completion. **Dual enrollment programs** enable high school students to begin healthcare training before graduation.

## The Core Tension: Future Investment vs. Current Transformation

### The Investment Horizon Problem

Youth programs produce returns over decades. **A health careers program starting in 2026 might graduate high school students in 2030, see them complete healthcare training by 2035, and establish rural practices by 2040.** The fifteen-year pipeline conflicts with RHTP's five-year funding period.

This creates a fundamental mismatch. RHTP metrics measure transformation during the funding period. Workforce shortages demand immediate solutions. State agencies face pressure to demonstrate current impact with current dollars. **Investments that pay off after 2030 appear in program reports as costs without benefits.**

Youth investment advocates argue that **sustainable transformation requires workforce development that extends beyond funding periods**. Current providers will retire. Current shortages will worsen without pipeline development. Focusing only on immediate transformation ignores the workforce crisis that will make transformation gains temporary.

Current transformation advocates argue that **communities need healthcare access now, not promises of future providers**. Every dollar spent on youth programs is a dollar not spent on current service delivery. Youth may complete training and leave for metropolitan areas regardless of rural pipeline programs. **Future-oriented investment is speculation with community health resources.**

### The School-Based Health Center Sustainability Question

SBHCs illustrate the tension differently. SBHCs deliver current services to current students: healthcare access today, not workforce development for tomorrow. **The question is whether SBHC expansion through RHTP creates sustainable infrastructure or temporary service expansion that disappears when funding ends.**

SBHC financial sustainability depends on multiple factors:

**Billing revenue:** SBHCs that can bill Medicaid, CHIP, and private insurance generate ongoing revenue. But billing covers only portion of costs, and many students lack billable coverage.

**State support:** States with dedicated SBHC funding streams (like Oregon, New York, or Colorado) provide stability that RHTP supplementation extends. States without such funding see SBHCs dependent on grants.

**Sponsoring organization capacity:** FQHC-sponsored SBHCs benefit from organization-wide infrastructure and 330 grant support. Hospital-sponsored SBHCs may access institutional resources. School-sponsored SBHCs often lack healthcare financing expertise.

**Community commitment:** SBHCs with strong community support may find local funding when federal grants end. SBHCs without such support may close.

RHTP can expand SBHC access during the funding period. **Whether expansion survives 2030 depends on factors RHTP cannot control**: state policy, sponsoring organization stability, and community resources.

### Vignette: The Health Careers Dilemma

Farmington Consolidated Schools serves 2,400 students across a rural county that has lost half its population since 1980. The county hospital closed in 2019. The remaining physician practice struggles to recruit, and the senior partner plans retirement in 2028. Without intervention, the county faces having no local physicians by 2030.

The school district proposes an RHTP-funded Health Careers Academy: intensive programming for high school students including healthcare curriculum, clinical shadowing at the 45-minute-away hospital, college counseling for healthcare programs, and scholarship support for students pursuing nursing or allied health. Estimated cost: $800,000 over four years.

Supporters argue the program addresses root causes. "We'll never recruit physicians to come here," the superintendent notes. "We have to grow our own. These kids know this community, love this community, and might actually come back."

Critics on the RHTP advisory committee counter that the program produces no providers before 2030. "We're facing a crisis now. In four years we might have no doctor at all. And there's no guarantee these kids come back. They might take their training to Denver and never look back."

The committee debates: Fund the Academy and hope for long-term returns? Or direct the $800,000 toward current access, perhaps a telehealth hub and nurse practitioner recruitment incentive, that delivers services during the funding period?

The compromise funds both, inadequately. The Academy receives $300,000, enough for program development but not full implementation. The telehealth hub receives $300,000, enough for equipment but not sustainable staffing. Neither investment achieves its potential.

Five years later, the Academy has produced 15 graduates pursuing healthcare careers, none of whom have returned to the county. The telehealth hub operates sporadically, dependent on grant renewals. The physician did retire, and the county now relies on a part-time nurse practitioner visiting twice weekly from the hospital 45 minutes away.

## Organizational Capacity Assessment

### School District Capacity

School districts have institutional capacity that many community organizations lack: established governance, professional administration, financial management systems, and employment infrastructure. **Districts routinely manage federal funds** through Title I, IDEA, school nutrition programs, and other education funding streams.

However, healthcare is not education's core competency. Districts operating SBHCs must develop healthcare expertise or partner with healthcare sponsors. Districts pursuing workforce programs must connect to healthcare systems and training institutions outside their normal relationships.

| Capacity Dimension | School Districts | 4-H/Extension | Other Youth Orgs |
|--------------------|-----------------|---------------|------------------|
| Organizational stability | High | High | Variable |
| Financial management | High | High | Variable |
| Federal compliance | High | High | Often limited |
| Community connection | High | Moderate to high | Variable |
| Healthcare expertise | Low | Low | Very low |
| Youth access | Very high | Moderate | Variable |
| Sustainability likelihood | Moderate | High | Low to moderate |

### SBHC Sponsor Capacity

SBHC capacity depends heavily on sponsoring organization:

**FQHC sponsors** bring healthcare expertise, billing infrastructure, compliance systems, and mission alignment. FQHCs increasingly sponsor rural SBHCs, bringing sustainability advantages their healthcare focus enables.

**Hospital sponsors** provide clinical expertise and potential service integration, but face their own financial pressures and may view SBHCs as community benefit obligations rather than strategic priorities.

**School district sponsors** have community commitment but often lack healthcare expertise. District-sponsored SBHCs may struggle with billing, clinical quality, and sustainability.

**Health department sponsors** bring public health perspective but face their own capacity constraints and political vulnerabilities.

### Youth Organization Capacity

**4-H/Extension** has unique capacity through its federal-state-local structure. Extension's presence in virtually every county provides infrastructure that national youth organizations cannot match in rural areas. Health programming capacity varies by state and county investment.

**Other youth organizations** have highly variable capacity. National organizations (Big Brothers Big Sisters, Boys & Girls Clubs) have organizational infrastructure but limited rural presence. Local organizations may have deep community connections but limited professional capacity.

## When Schools/Youth Organizations Can Support Transformation

**School-based health centers delivering current services** represent the clearest transformation contribution. SBHCs with sustainable funding models, strong sponsoring organizations, and community support can expand healthcare access during and potentially beyond RHTP funding periods.

**Health education improving current behaviors** produces immediate benefits even if long-term effects are uncertain. Nutrition education reducing obesity, substance use prevention reducing addiction, and mental health literacy reducing crisis all deliver current value.

**School facilities enabling healthcare access** provide infrastructure benefits. Using school buildings for mobile clinic sites, vaccination events, or community health programming leverages existing assets without requiring healthcare infrastructure investment.

**Workforce programs with accelerated timelines** can produce some returns within RHTP periods. Medical assistant certification programs, CNA training, or community health worker preparation can place workers in healthcare roles within two to three years, faster than traditional professional pathways.

## When Schools/Youth Organizations Cannot Support Transformation

**Long-term workforce pipeline programs** producing results after 2030 cannot demonstrate transformation impact within RHTP metrics. They may be valuable investments but require funding sources designed for long-term returns.

**Unsustainable SBHC expansion** creates temporary services that disappear when funding ends. Expansion without sustainability planning may harm communities by establishing expectations that cannot be maintained.

**Youth programs without healthcare connection** provide youth development benefits but do not constitute health transformation. Funding generic youth programs through RHTP diverts resources from health-specific activities.

**Schools lacking healthcare capacity or partners** cannot develop healthcare functions quickly enough to deliver transformation within funding periods. Capacity building takes time that RHTP's timeline does not provide.

## Alternative Perspective: The Future Investment View

The transformation framework may be wrong. **Sustainable rural health requires workforce that does not currently exist.** Focusing only on current transformation accepts workforce constraints that make long-term improvement impossible.

**The Future Investment Argument:** RHTP's $50 billion over five years cannot solve workforce shortages created over decades. Young people leaving rural communities for education and careers represent lost investment that no recruitment incentive recovers. The only sustainable solution is developing healthcare workers from within rural communities, people who know the places, have family connections, and might actually stay.

This requires investment horizons longer than funding periods. **Health careers programs in middle schools, high school academies, community college partnerships, and return-to-practice scholarships** create pipelines that produce workers a decade later. Current transformation without workforce development is temporary improvement that erodes when current workers retire.

**Counter-Argument:** Future-oriented investment is speculation. Youth completing healthcare training have opportunities everywhere. Nothing binds them to rural communities that funded their development. Meanwhile, communities lacking current services experience current harm that future workforce cannot address. **Hope is not strategy.**

**Assessment:** Both perspectives have validity. **Workforce development is necessary for sustainable transformation** but insufficient for current crisis. RHTP should include workforce pipeline components while recognizing their returns arrive after funding ends. Metrics should track both current service delivery and pipeline development, accepting that some investments produce long-term rather than short-term returns.

## Recommendations

**For Schools:**
- Pursue SBHC development with sustainable sponsoring organizations
- Emphasize current service delivery contributions rather than future workforce alone
- Partner with healthcare organizations for clinical expertise
- Document health impact on educational outcomes
- Integrate health careers into career and technical education

**For Youth Organizations:**
- Connect existing programming to health workforce pathways
- Partner with healthcare employers for internships and mentorship
- Develop accelerated training pathways producing workers within 2-3 years
- Document health-related outcomes of youth programming
- Advocate for funding timelines appropriate to workforce development

**For State Agencies:**
- Include SBHC expansion in RHTP where sustainable funding exists
- Fund workforce pipeline components accepting long-term returns
- Require sustainability planning for school-based initiatives
- Measure workforce pipeline development alongside current transformation
- Partner with education agencies for school-health integration

**For Healthcare Partners:**
- Sponsor SBHCs to bring healthcare expertise to school settings
- Provide clinical rotation and mentorship opportunities for health careers programs
- Value schools as community institutions, not just workforce sources
- Support sustainable SBHC financing through Medicaid participation
- Develop "grow your own" partnerships with local schools

**For CMS:**
- Allow workforce pipeline investments recognizing returns beyond funding period
- Require SBHC sustainability assessment before funding expansion
- Support measurement systems capturing long-term workforce development
- Provide guidance on school-health partnerships within RHTP
- Recognize education-health connections in transformation metrics
