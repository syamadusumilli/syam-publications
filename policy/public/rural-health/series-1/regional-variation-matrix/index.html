Read the Executive Summary

National rural statistics obscure dramatic regional variation. A county in rural Vermont shares little with a county in the Mississippi Delta beyond federal classification. Policy designed for “rural America” as monolith fails communities whose challenges differ fundamentally.

This reference document provides systematic comparison across seven primary rural regions on five dimensions:

  1. Demographics
  2. Economics
  3. Healthcare infrastructure
  4. Social infrastructure
  5. Health outcomes

The matrix establishes baseline regional profiles supporting Series 10 (Regional Deep Dives) and enabling region-appropriate policy analysis throughout the project.

Part I: Regional Definitions#

Seven Primary Rural Regions#

Region Geographic Scope Defining Characteristics
Appalachia 423 counties across 13 states from NY to MS Mountain geography, extraction economy legacy, cultural distinctiveness
Delta MS, AR, LA river counties plus adjacent areas Plantation legacy, persistent poverty, majority-minority demographics
Black Belt South AL, GA, SC, NC crescent of historically high Black population Agricultural legacy, out-migration, institutional disinvestment
Great Plains TX Panhandle through Dakotas Agricultural consolidation, extreme depopulation, vast distances
Rural West Mountain and intermountain states excluding Pacific coast metros Federal land dominance, tribal nations, amenity migration
Upper Midwest WI, MN, MI, northern IL, IN, OH rural Dairy, manufacturing decline, aging Scandinavian/German heritage
New England Rural ME, NH, VT, western MA, CT, RI rural Small towns, tourism dependency, aging population, high costs

Regional Overlap#

Regions are analytical constructs, not exclusive categories. Key overlaps:

  • Appalachia and Rural South share counties in TN, KY, NC, VA, WV
  • Delta and Black Belt share persistent poverty characteristics
  • Great Plains and Rural West share distance and federal land presence
  • Upper Midwest and Appalachia share post-industrial transition in OH, PA

Part VII: Regional Summary Profiles#

Appalachia#

Defining challenge: Post-extraction economic collapse without replacement industry

Healthcare crisis: Hospital closures, workforce exodus, opioid epidemic epicenter

Demographic pressure: Out-migration of working-age adults, aging in place without services

Political context: Deep distrust of federal government, strong cultural identity, suspicion of outside intervention

RHTP relevance: High funding need, moderate-high allocation, implementation challenged by workforce absence

Delta (Mississippi/Arkansas/Louisiana)#

Defining challenge: Persistent poverty rooted in plantation economy legacy

Healthcare crisis: Worst health outcomes nationally, Medicaid non-expansion, severe provider shortage

Demographic pressure: Continued out-migration, aging population, majority-minority demographics

Political context: State governments historically resistant to federal health programs

RHTP relevance: Critical need, allocations constrained by state capacity and political will

Black Belt South#

Defining challenge: Historical disinvestment in majority-Black communities

Healthcare crisis: Hospital closures concentrated, maternal mortality crisis, specialty access absent

Demographic pressure: Young adult out-migration, elderly population growth, caregiving burden

Political context: Variation by state, some expansion (LA) vs. non-expansion (GA, AL)

RHTP relevance: High need, implementation dependent on state-level decisions

Great Plains#

Defining challenge: Agricultural consolidation and depopulation creating service deserts

Healthcare crisis: Distance rather than absence, CAH network stretched thin, EMS response times

Demographic pressure: Rapid aging, schools closing, communities below sustainability threshold

Political context: Strong self-reliance ethos, skepticism of government programs

RHTP relevance: Distance bonus in formula helps, but population loss undermines infrastructure viability

Rural West#

Defining challenge: Bifurcation between amenity-rich areas and struggling communities

Healthcare crisis: Tribal health disparities, frontier isolation, seasonal population swings

Demographic pressure: Amenity migration creating housing cost pressure, tribal communities often youngest

Political context: Libertarian threads, federal land tension, tribal sovereignty considerations

RHTP relevance: IHS coordination critical, state variation significant

Upper Midwest#

Defining challenge: Manufacturing decline without complete collapse

Healthcare crisis: Moderate but growing, workforce aging faster than replacement

Demographic pressure: Stable but slowly declining, immigration partially offsetting native out-migration

Political context: Strong civic infrastructure, cooperative traditions, pragmatic politics

RHTP relevance: Relatively strong implementation capacity, moderate need

New England Rural#

Defining challenge: High costs and aging population in otherwise functional systems

Healthcare crisis: Least severe nationally, but workforce aging and facility updates needed

Demographic pressure: Oldest population, in-migration of retirees, limited young family formation

Political context: Universal Medicaid expansion, strong state health departments

RHTP relevance: Lower formula allocation due to better baseline, implementation capacity strong

Part VIII: Cross-Regional Analysis#

Regions Requiring Intensive RHTP Investment#

Rank Region Rationale
1 Delta Worst outcomes, weakest infrastructure, lowest capacity
2 Black Belt South Comparable crisis to Delta, slightly better state capacity in some areas
3 Appalachia Severe crisis with workforce absence, opioid overlay
4 Great Plains Distance challenges unique, depopulation threatens all infrastructure
5 Rural West Bifurcated needs, tribal health requires distinct approach
6 Upper Midwest Moderate need, strong implementation potential
7 New England Rural Lowest need, highest capacity

Regional Variation in Transformation Approach#

Approach Best Fit Regions Poor Fit Regions
Telehealth expansion Great Plains, Rural West Delta, Appalachia (broadband limits)
Hub-and-spoke networks Upper Midwest, New England Great Plains (distance), Delta (hub absence)
Community health workers Delta, Black Belt, Appalachia Great Plains (population density)
Workforce loan repayment All regions Effectiveness varies by baseline
Hospital conversion (REH) Appalachia, Black Belt Great Plains (distance makes ER-only risky)
Mobile health units Great Plains, Rural West New England (less needed)

Part IX: Data Sources and Methodology#

Primary Data Sources#

  • Demographics: Census Bureau ACS 5-year estimates, Population Estimates Program
  • Economics: BEA Regional Economic Accounts, BLS QCEW, USDA ERS
  • Healthcare: AHRF, AHA Annual Survey, CMS Provider Data, UNC Sheps Center
  • Health Outcomes: CDC WONDER, NVSS, BRFSS
  • Social Infrastructure: USDA Food Access Atlas, FCC Broadband Maps, various surveys

Regional Boundary Sources#

  • Appalachia: Appalachian Regional Commission official designation
  • Delta: Delta Regional Authority designation plus adjacent high-poverty counties
  • Black Belt: Historical definition plus persistent poverty overlay
  • Great Plains: USDA ERS farm-dependent counties plus adjacent
  • Rural West: Mountain and intermountain nonmetro counties
  • Upper Midwest: State-based definition of rural counties in WI, MN, MI, northern tier
  • New England: State-based definition of nonmetro counties

Limitations#

  • Regional boundaries are analytical constructs with inherent arbitrariness
  • Within-region variation often exceeds between-region variation
  • Data vintage varies by source (2020-2024)
  • Some metrics unavailable at sub-state regional level
