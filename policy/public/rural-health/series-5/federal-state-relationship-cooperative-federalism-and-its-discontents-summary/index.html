The Rural Health Transformation Program describes itself as a cooperative agreement, language suggesting partnership and mutual respect. The legal reality diverges sharply from this framing. CMS controls $50 billion in federal funding while states bear implementation responsibility without commensurate authority. This structural asymmetry defines every interaction, from initial application requirements through quarterly reporting obligations to the perpetual threat of clawback for underperformance. The relationship is not a partnership of equals, and acknowledging this reality represents the essential first step toward making the arrangement function despite its inherent tensions.

The fundamental disagreement concerns who understands rural health challenges and who should control transformation strategy. CMS officials carry legitimate accountability concerns about the largest rural health investment in American history. Congressional appropriators, GAO auditors, and administration officials will scrutinize every expenditure, and when state programs fail or funds disappear into administrative overhead, federal officials face consequences despite not making implementation decisions. Federal officials who witnessed state mismanagement of ACA marketplace implementation and Medicaid waiver authority have experiential reasons for skepticism about state assurances. Uniform standards enable cross-state performance comparison and prevent states from hiding underperformance behind claims of unique circumstances. Federal oversight protects against state capture by hospital associations and provider interests that dominate many state capitals. Without federal mandates pushing transformation, states might simply subsidize incumbent providers without demanding meaningful change.

State officials implementing RHTP carry equally legitimate frustrations rooted in ground-level reality. Texas border counties differ fundamentally from Alaska Native villages, Appalachian Kentucky differs from California’s Central Valley, and North Dakota wheat country bears no resemblance to Mississippi Delta cotton land. Rigid federal requirements force states into templates that ignore local conditions, imposing solutions that work in Minnesota on Arizona contexts where they fail predictably. Every hour spent preparing quarterly reports, documenting compliance, and justifying expenditures represents an hour unavailable for actual transformation work. States with skeletal rural health offices face impossible tradeoffs between meeting federal requirements and doing the work those requirements supposedly enable. Approval processes introduce weeks or months of delay when states need to modify subawards or redirect resources in response to provider closures or workforce departures. The 21-month obligation deadline creates urgency that federal approval timelines directly contradict. States cannot demonstrate agility while waiting for CMS authorization on every significant decision.

The evidence question matters more than ideological positions about federalism. Section 1115 waivers have enabled genuine innovation in states with capacity and commitment, demonstrating that federal flexibility can improve implementation. The Flex Program’s relatively light federal oversight has allowed states to adapt Critical Access Hospital support to local conditions with documented success. Simultaneously, other states given autonomy have squandered opportunities, using waiver authority to restrict coverage, impose barriers, and serve political rather than health objectives. Block grant proposals have historically preceded spending cuts rather than innovation. Implementation science suggests the optimal balance varies by function: core performance metrics may benefit from standardization while implementation approaches benefit from flexibility, and compliance processes may burden states without producing meaningful accountability benefits.

The cooperative agreement instrument requires substantial federal involvement in implementation through assigned project officers, technical assistance providers, stakeholder meeting participation, and ongoing engagement throughout the five-year program period. This involvement is not optional or negotiable. States that attempt to minimize federal engagement violate their award terms. The partnership language obscures fundamental power realities that shape every interaction. CMS holds funding authority, meaning states cannot spend RHTP money without federal approval for major expenditure categories, subaward structures, and budget modifications. CMS defines compliance standards and determines whether states meet requirements, with ambiguous situations resolving according to federal interpretation. CMS controls consequences for poor performance, deciding what constitutes underperformance and imposing enhanced monitoring, additional reporting requirements, or funding clawback. States can appeal or disagree, but neither prevents consequences from proceeding.

Technical assistance infrastructure illustrates the inherent ambiguity of federal involvement. CMS has contracted with organizations possessing genuine expertise in telehealth reimbursement, workforce pipeline design, hub-and-spoke network structure, and evidence-based practice implementation. For states that want help, technical assistance provides valuable access to experts that under-resourced rural health offices could not otherwise afford. States learn from peers facing similar challenges, and implementation quality improves. For states that feel monitored rather than supported, the same technical assistance functions as surveillance. Technical assistance providers report to CMS, meaning information shared in supposedly helpful conversations may appear in federal performance assessments. States that reveal implementation challenges risk triggering enhanced oversight, creating incentives to present success narratives rather than seeking genuine help. The technical assistance itself is identical across both scenarios; the relationship context transforms its meaning completely.

A Critical Access Hospital closure in rural Montana and an identical closure in rural Georgia during the same week produced radically different federal responses despite both states following appropriate processes. Montana’s program director called her CMS project officer immediately, outlined three options for redirecting affected funds, and received a suggested fourth option within the conversation. Informal approval arrived within one week, allowing implementation to begin immediately while formal modification paperwork proceeded through standard channels. Georgia’s administrator submitted a formal modification request through the CMS portal after learning of the closure from local news reports. Three weeks passed without response. Six weeks after the closure announcement, Georgia received conditional approval with additional reporting requirements. The affected communities experienced two months without clear direction while awaiting federal authorization. The difference was relationship quality rather than procedures or compliance. Montana’s collaborative relationship enabled rapid problem-solving; Georgia’s compliance-focused relationship imposed delays that directly harmed communities RHTP exists to serve.

Federal oversight mechanisms establish multiple accountability layers that consume significant state capacity. Project officers serve as primary federal contacts, with effectiveness varying dramatically based on individual judgment and relationship quality. Effective project officers understand state contexts, facilitate problem-solving, and provide early warning when issues arise. Ineffective project officers enforce requirements without contextual judgment, creating friction that hampers implementation without improving accountability. Quarterly progress reports document activities against plan milestones, financial expenditures, emerging challenges, and course corrections using standardized templates enabling cross-state comparison. States estimate these reports require 40 to 80 staff hours to prepare, time directly unavailable for implementation activities. Annual performance reviews assess whether states are meeting objectives through quantitative metrics and qualitative implementation assessment, with poor performance triggering consequences from enhanced monitoring to clawback. The annual Rural Health Summit convening state leaders and federal officials can facilitate genuine peer learning or devolve into performance theater depending on CMS structuring decisions.

Federal flexibility exists in subaward structures, where states can direct funds through intermediary organizations, make direct awards to providers, or combine approaches with CMS generally deferring to state judgment. The ten approved use categories encompass most rural health transformation activities, with the tenth category providing catch-all authority for innovative approaches promoting sustainable rural health access. Timeline adjustments are possible for states facing legitimate implementation delays, with approval not automatic but CMS showing willingness to accommodate realistic modifications. Technical assistance is formally optional in practice, with states able to engage deeply or maintain minimal contact despite some federal engagement being required. Other requirements permit no negotiation whatsoever. Obligation deadlines are statutory, reflecting Congressional mandate that CMS cannot waive; states that fail to obligate FY2026 funds by September 30, 2027 lose those resources permanently. Prohibited uses are absolute, with RHTP unable to backfill Medicaid cuts, provide operating subsidies, or fund maintenance rather than transformation based on statutory language. Performance metrics are standardized, with states able to propose additional measures reflecting local priorities but required to report on CMS-specified core indicators regardless of whether those measures capture what matters locally.

Federal-state relationships cluster into recognizable patterns reflecting history, leadership, capacity, and political context. Collaborative relationships characterized by mutual trust, proactive communication, and joint problem-solving generally produce high implementation quality, found in states with prior CMS partnership success and experienced rural health leadership. Compliance-focused relationships emphasizing rule-following and formal communication produce adequate but uninspired implementation, common in states with limited capacity, risk-averse leadership, or recent compliance issues. Adversarial relationships marked by tension, contested interpretations, and legalistic engagement produce variable and often poor implementation quality, appearing in states with political conflicts with the federal administration or prior disputes. Developing relationships represent new engagements with trajectory unclear and quality still being established. Prior program experience shapes expectations, with states that had positive ACA implementation experiences bringing trust into RHTP engagement while states that battled CMS over waiver interpretations bring wariness based on learned behavior. Leadership matters enormously: state rural health directors with CMS relationships built over decades navigate federal systems that mystify newer colleagues, with personal relationships between state and federal officials accelerating problem-solving and relationship quality often shifting when key personnel change.

The federalism critique deserves serious engagement despite substantial counterarguments. Federal requirements reflect CMS needs for standardization, accountability, and political protection rather than necessarily serving rural health transformation. Requirements designed to prevent worst cases may actively harm best cases. States estimate that 15 to 25 percent of RHTP administrative effort goes to federal reporting and compliance rather than implementation, with this burden falling most heavily on small states with limited staff that can least afford compliance overhead
