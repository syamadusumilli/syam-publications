Read the Executive Summary

Tribal health complexity meets Medicaid vulnerability: $167 million for 800,000 rural Arizonans across 113,000 square miles of desert, mountain, and plateau.

Introduction#

Arizona received $166,988,956 for FY2026, placing it among the lowest state allocations nationally alongside New Jersey, Connecticut, Rhode Island, Delaware, Massachusetts, and Maryland. The five-year projection approaches $835 million. This positioning reflects the RHTP funding formula’s structural bias toward geographic sparsity over population concentration, but Arizona’s challenges extend far beyond funding levels.

The state applied for $200 million annually and received roughly 83% of that request. Governor Katie Hobbs’s office accused the Trump administration of “shortchanging rural Arizonans,” and the state has submitted a public records request seeking insight into CMS scoring methodology. Arizona submitted a downward budget modification after receiving less than requested, with revised budget due by January 30, 2026.

For the nearly 800,000 Arizonans living in rural and tribal communities, RHTP represents opportunity constrained by deeper structural challenges: tribal health systems operating under distinct federal frameworks, Medicaid coverage for one in four state residents facing projected cuts, and workforce shortages that no five-year program can resolve.

Rural Health Landscape#

Geographic Reality

Arizona spans 113,990 square miles of Sonoran Desert, mountain ranges, and high plateau country. The state’s 7.3 million residents cluster overwhelmingly in the Phoenix and Tucson metropolitan areas, leaving vast rural territory with sparse population but significant health needs.

By the Census Bureau’s nonmetro designation, only 340,160 Arizonans (4.7% of the state) live in rural areas. This narrow definition understates the scope of Arizona’s rural health challenge. The state health department identifies 15 rural counties requiring transformation, and the Arizona Center for Rural Health documents substantial rural populations even within counties classified as metropolitan. Using the broader designation of rural and tribal communities, approximately 800,000 Arizonans live in areas served by RHTP initiatives.

The Arizona Center for Rural Health at the University of Arizona serves as the State Office of Rural Health. The Arizona Rural Hospital Flexibility Program (AzFlex) provides technical assistance to Critical Access Hospitals.

The AZ REACH program (Arizona Resource Equity and Access Coordination Hub), launched in December 2022, supports patient transfers from CAHs, IHS facilities, and tribally operated hospitals. Within three months of launch, over 1,000 patients had been transferred through the coordinated system.

Tribal Health Dominates

Arizona has the third largest American Indian population among all states. The state’s rural population is approximately 15% Native American, and more than half of Arizona’s 15 counties include reservation lands. The state recognizes 22 federally recognized tribes, operates through 21 tribal health offices, and coordinates with three Indian Health Service Area Offices.

Tribal health facilities operate under distinct federal frameworks:

  • Indian Health Service direct care facilities
  • Tribally operated 638 facilities under P.L. 93-638 self-determination contracts
  • Urban Indian Health Programs serving AI/AN populations in Phoenix and Tucson
  • Dual-designated facilities operating as both Tribal 638 and FQHC

Apache County recorded the highest excess death rate of any large county nationwide in both 2020 and 2021, while neighboring Navajo County had the second and fourth highest rates in those years. The pandemic devastated Arizona’s tribal communities, revealing infrastructure gaps that RHTP funding cannot quickly remedy.

Workforce Crisis

Arizona experiences primary care physician shortages in all counties, with rural areas substantially worse. The ratio of population to primary care providers stands at 2,407:1 in urban areas versus 3,896:1 in rural Arizona.

Almost 40% of Arizonans live in a Health Professional Shortage Area. The state needs 665 additional practitioners to remove these federal designations. Eighty-two Arizona Medically Underserved Areas have been designated, with 12 falling within tribal nations’ lands.

The White Mountain and San Carlos Apache Reservations each carry HPSA scores of 21, the highest among Arizona’s tribal nations and indicating severe shortage conditions.

Border Health Dimension

Arizona shares 370 miles of border with Mexico. Border counties face distinct health challenges including environmental factors, cross-border population movement, and infrastructure limitations. Santa Cruz County sees more than 40% of residents relying on Medicaid coverage, while employer-based insurance remains far less prevalent than in Phoenix or Tucson.

Arizona’s workload allocation fell significantly short of the baseline, indicating that CMS scoring factors did not favor the state’s application. Technical scoring analysis by the University of North Carolina’s Sheps Center placed Arizona among the lowest technical award recipients alongside New Mexico, Michigan, New Jersey, and California.

Medicaid Context

Arizona expanded Medicaid in 2014 through the Arizona Health Care Cost Containment System (AHCCCS, pronounced “access”). Current enrollment exceeds 2 million people, representing nearly one in four Arizona residents.

A Georgetown University study in January 2026 showed rural Arizona communities have the nation’s highest rates of adults covered by Medicaid and among the highest rates of children covered by the program. Enrollment is especially high among Arizona’s Native American communities and the state’s large population of older adults.

Medicaid plays a disproportionately important role in rural Arizona. More than 40% of residents in Apache, Graham, Navajo, and Santa Cruz Counties rely on AHCCCS coverage. In several rural counties, Medicaid enrollment exceeds employer-based coverage.

The Medicaid Cut Reality

The Kaiser Family Foundation estimates more than 350,000 Arizonans will lose AHCCCS coverage by 2034 as a result of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act’s work requirements and changes to federal funding. The Joint Economic Committee of Congress estimates approximately 190,000 Arizonans will lose coverage due to documentation requirements alone.

Arizona has a trigger law that would automatically repeal Medicaid expansion if federal matching drops below 80%. The childless adult population (approximately 450,000 people under Proposition 204) faces particular exposure, as does the expansion population covered at the 90% enhanced federal match rate.

The projected $34 billion in Arizona Medicaid cuts over 10 years dwarfs the $835 million RHTP investment. As Governor Hobbs’s health policy advisor noted, no matter how much Medicaid is cut, the same number of people will get sick and require care.

State Approach#

Governance Structure

Governor Katie Hobbs designated the Arizona Health Care Cost Containment System (AHCCCS) as the lead agency to apply for and administer RHTP funding. The Arizona Department of Health Services (ADHS) serves as co-lead for specific initiatives.

Arizona conducted stakeholder engagement through:

  • Statewide Request for Information through the Department of Administration
  • Public webinars on October 30 and November 13, 2025
  • Collaboration with rural providers, Medicaid health plans, tribal partners, and community leaders

The state is establishing a central RHTP Program Office and hiring key staff to build the program and ensure coordination, accountability, and compliance.

Strategic Framework

Arizona organized its approach around four strategic themes addressing the most urgent health needs in rural Arizona:

1. Making Rural Healthcare Accessible (Lead Agency: AHCCCS)

  • Expanding telehealth services and deploying mobile units and satellite sites
  • Providing grants to modernize service delivery and expand clinical capabilities
  • Piloting innovative care and alternative payment models
  • Investing in secure, modern digital health systems for care coordination

2. Strengthening Rural Healthcare Resiliency (Lead Agency: AHCCCS)

  • Creating a rural provider liaison pilot program within AHCCCS, DIFI, and ADHS
  • Supporting core operating costs for rural clinics and hospitals transitioning to co-located facilities
  • Delivering technical assistance to improve operational performance and viability
  • Building shared back-office services to simplify care delivery and reduce overhead

3. Growing the Rural Healthcare Workforce (Lead Agency: Governor’s Office of Economic Opportunity)

  • Establishing new rural clinical rotations and residency slots at multiple universities
  • Expanding accelerated education programs
  • Implementing State Loan Repayment Program expansion
  • Career and technical education programs targeting rural students

4. Priority Health Initiatives Grants Portfolio (Lead Agency: AHCCCS and ADHS)

  • Behavioral health and substance use services ($10 million per year)
  • Maternal and infant health initiatives
  • Chronic disease management programs
  • Preventive care expansion

Arizona committed to joining the Physician Assistant Compact and EMS Compact by 2028, representing policy change that persists beyond RHTP and requires no allocation.

SDOH/HRSN Approach

Arizona’s RHTP application does not explicitly center social determinants of health screening or health-related social needs navigation as distinct initiatives. Social care elements are embedded within broader access and care coordination investments rather than receiving dedicated funding streams.

Community Health Worker Programs

CHW deployment exists primarily within tribal health contexts. AHCCCS funds training of Community Health Representatives (CHRs) and peer support specialists, many of whom are tribal members. These individuals act as liaisons between providers and community members. RHTP workforce investments may expand CHW training but do not establish a dedicated CHW deployment initiative.

Technology and Referral Infrastructure

Arizona’s Health Information Exchange is expanding to include tribal clinics, allowing better coordination between primary care, behavioral health, and emergency services. The application describes plans to secure vendors for data infrastructure but does not specify closed-loop referral systems or CIE platform procurement.

Traditional Healing (Non-RHTP)

AHCCCS launched Medicaid-covered Traditional Healing services beginning October 1, 2025, separate from RHTP. This historic step affirms culturally responsive care and honors Indigenous healing traditions. While not RHTP-funded, this initiative demonstrates Arizona’s attention to culturally appropriate care delivery.

Assessment#

Strengths

Medicaid Expansion Foundation. Arizona expanded Medicaid in 2014 and maintained expansion across multiple administrations. The insurance coverage baseline reduces access challenges compared to non-expansion states. Rural providers have more favorable payer mix despite heavy Medicaid dependence.

Tribal Health Infrastructure. Arizona has existing mechanisms for tribal coordination that most states lack. The AZ REACH transfer system, Traditional Healing coverage, and CHR training programs demonstrate state capacity to engage tribal health systems.

Workforce Policy Commitments. The commitment to PA and EMS compacts by 2028 represents durable policy change that persists indefinitely at no RHTP cost. Workforce pipeline investments in rotations and residencies can yield practitioners who remain in Arizona rural practice beyond 2030.

Data Infrastructure Focus. Arizona’s emphasis on building secure data pipelines, dashboards, and fiscal tracking tools addresses CMS compliance requirements while creating permanent infrastructure. These investments survive the 2030 cliff.

Administrative Efficiency. The plan targets administrative costs below 3%, maximizing resources flowing to programs and providers.

Vulnerabilities

Tribal Health Complexity. Arizona’s dominant rural health challenge operates largely outside RHTP’s direct reach. IHS facilities, 638 programs, and urban Indian health centers maintain distinct funding streams, governance structures, and accountability mechanisms. RHTP funding flows through the state to providers, but tribal health facilities may or may not participate depending on sovereignty considerations and federal coordination requirements.

Medicaid Exposure. Rural providers in Arizona depend heavily on Medicaid revenue. The projected loss of 190,000 to 350,000 AHCCCS enrollees will increase uncompensated care at hospitals and stress facilities already operating on thin margins. RHTP investments improve infrastructure for a population increasingly unable to afford the care that infrastructure provides.

Low Technical Score. Arizona ranked among the lowest technical award recipients, receiving less discretionary funding than most states. Future workload allocations may shift based on demonstrated progress, but the state begins from a disadvantaged position.

Application Detail Gaps. The application’s limited detail on tribal engagement mechanisms and social care integration suggests these areas received less development time. Implementation will require substantial additional planning.

Behavioral Health Workforce. The $10 million annual behavioral health investment cannot overcome fundamental workforce shortages. Arizona lacks sufficient behavioral health providers to staff integrated models regardless of available financing. Training programs help at the margin but cannot produce psychiatrists or psychiatric nurse practitioners at the scale Arizona’s rural areas require.

Sustainability Prospects

Durable Investments. Workforce pipeline investments, PA and EMS compact participation, and data infrastructure represent permanent changes that survive federal funding expiration. These create lasting value.

Operational Dependency. Mobile clinics, telehealth hubs, and provider support programs require ongoing operational funding. Without revenue models that survive 2030 or state general fund commitment, these face discontinuation risk.

The Medicaid Variable. Arizona’s Medicaid trigger law creates risk that dwarfs RHTP considerations. If federal matching for expansion populations drops below 80%, state law mandates automatic repeal. This would remove insurance from over 500,000 Arizonans and devastate rural provider finances regardless of RHTP investment.

Critical Success Factors

  1. Budget revision acceptance: CMS must approve the revised budget by early 2026 for implementation to proceed on schedule
  2. Tribal engagement: Meaningful participation by tribal health systems, not just nominal inclusion
  3. Workforce pipeline execution: Clinical rotation and residency expansion must produce measurable provider increases
  4. Medicaid stability: Coverage levels must remain stable for RHTP investments to yield proportionate benefit
  5. Compact implementation: PA and EMS compact participation by 2028 as committed

Social Care Integration Viability

Social care integration faces uncertain viability in Arizona. Without dedicated SDOH/HRSN funding streams, closed-loop referral infrastructure, or explicit CHW deployment targets, social care elements remain embedded within broader initiatives and vulnerable to implementation triage. The 83% award level may force prioritization of core access and workforce investments over social care components.

Conclusion#

Arizona’s $167 million annual RHTP allocation is adequate for the state’s proposed workforce and telehealth initiatives. The harder truth is that Arizona’s rural health challenges concentrate in tribal communities and Medicaid-dependent populations where RHTP funding operates at the margins.

Tribal health requires coordination across sovereignty boundaries that money cannot purchase. The parallel infrastructure of IHS, 638 facilities, and state systems creates coordination challenges that persist regardless of RHTP investment levels. The 665 practitioners needed to eliminate HPSA designations will not materialize from workforce incentives alone.

Medicaid coverage depends on federal matching rates that RHTP cannot influence. The projected $34 billion in Arizona Medicaid cuts over 10 years makes the $835 million RHTP investment look like the bandage it is. If Medicaid erodes, RHTP investments improve infrastructure for a population increasingly unable to afford the care that infrastructure provides. If Medicaid holds, RHTP investments layer onto a stable coverage foundation.

Arizona will implement its plan competently. AHCCCS has experience administering large federal healthcare programs. The tribal health infrastructure, while complex, provides mechanisms for engagement that other states must build from scratch. Workforce investments and policy commitments represent genuine long-term value.

But whether rural health improves depends on variables beyond the state’s control: federal Medicaid policy, tribal sovereignty considerations, and national provider supply constraints. Arizona’s RHTP represents meaningful investment constrained by deeper structural challenges the program was never designed to solve.
