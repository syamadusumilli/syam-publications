South Central/Western Priority Region (Batch 9)#

Regional Overview#

The South Central/Western Priority states (TX, OK, CA) present dramatically different social care infrastructure paradigms reflecting expansion status, state capacity, and philosophical orientation toward health transformation. Texas demonstrates mature CHW certification without sustainable Medicaid financing combined with fragmented regional CIE deployments despite having the nation’s largest rural population. Oklahoma presents an emerging opportunity with 2024 SPA approval creating CHW reimbursement against minimal CIE infrastructure. California operates in a fundamentally different paradigm where CalAIM’s comprehensive 1115 waiver supersedes traditional CIE models while CHW workforce development remains constrained by paused certification and inadequate reimbursement rates.

Key Regional Patterns:

  1. Non-expansion states fragment social care: Texas and Oklahoma demonstrate MCO-driven approaches without state coordination infrastructure
  2. Scale creates market opportunity: Texas’s massive rural population (4.96M) creates platform opportunity despite fragmentation; no vendor has achieved statewide coordination
  3. CHW certification does not guarantee financing: Texas has 20+ year certification history but limited Medicaid reimbursement
  4. SPA development accelerating: Oklahoma’s 2024 SPA approval demonstrates national trend toward CHW Medicaid coverage
  5. CalAIM creates distinct market dynamics: California’s comprehensive transformation closes platform market; entry requires MCP partnership
  6. Tribal health creates parallel system: Oklahoma’s 39 federally recognized tribes operate CHR programs independently

Texas: Highest South Central/Western CIE Opportunity#

Research Date: January 29, 2026 Overall Status: PARTIAL (CHW certified but underfinanced, CIE fragmented) Confidence: High

RHTP Context

Metric Value

Rural Population ~4.96 million
Per Capita ~$104/rural resident

Texas holds the largest rural population of any state and the largest population in the coverage gap, creating extreme SDOH burden that RHTP cannot address without expansion. The scale penalty in per-capita funding ($104) compared to smaller states reflects formula structures rewarding geographic sparsity over population size.

CIE Platform Status: FRAGMENTED REGIONAL

Factor Status
Statewide Platform None
Findhelp Presence Yes (11 hospital systems, 8 health centers, Superior Health Plan MCO)
Unite Us Presence Yes (UMC Lubbock/South Plains Food Bank partnership)
Closed-Loop Referrals Regional only; no statewide interoperability
RHTP Investment Not explicitly included

What Exists:

Findhelp Deployments:

  • 11 hospital systems using platform for social needs screening and referral
  • 8 federally qualified health centers with active deployments
  • Superior Health Plan (Medicaid MCO) enterprise contract
  • ConnectATX Navigator Model: United Way Greater Austin partnership combines Findhelp platform with trained navigators
  • Statewide hospital system uptake but fragmented implementation

Unite Us Deployments:

  • UMC Lubbock partnership with South Plains Food Bank for regional coordination
  • Limited presence compared to Findhelp in major metro areas
  • No statewide network identified

Central Texas Model Community:

  • Austin-area integration combining Findhelp + Texas 211 + Connxus HIE + Dell Medical SHIP platform
  • Multi-platform coordination pilot without statewide replication

211 Texas:

  • United Way-operated statewide resource directory
  • Information and referral capability
  • No closed-loop functionality

CHCS May 2025 Report Findings:

  • Documented fragmented landscape requiring interoperability standards
  • Multiple platforms serving same geographies without data exchange
  • MCO-specific tools creating additional fragmentation

CHW Program Status: MATURE CERTIFICATION / LIMITED MEDICAID

Factor Status
State Certification Yes (Mandatory since 2001)
Certifying Body Texas DSHS CHW Training & Certification Program
Training Requirements 160-hour training OR 1,000 hours experience (past 3 years)
Training Programs 49 certified programs (community colleges, AHECs, FQHCs, CBOs)
Medicaid Reimbursement Limited

Certification Framework:

Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 48 (effective 2001) establishes mandatory certification for compensated CHWs:

  • No person may hold themselves out as a CHW or promotora for compensation without DSHS certification
  • Among the earliest and most comprehensive state certification requirements nationally
  • 49 approved training programs distributed across the state
  • Workforce estimated at 89% female with strong promotora tradition

Medicaid Reimbursement Limitations:

Texas lacks comprehensive SPA for CHW services. Limited coverage exists through targeted legislation:

HB 113 (2023):

  • Allows Medicaid MCOs to categorize CHW expenditures as quality improvement costs
  • Does not establish direct service reimbursement
  • Value-added benefit rather than covered service

HB 1575 (2023):

  • Establishes CHW as separate provider type in Medicaid
  • Limited to Case Management for Children & Pregnant Women Program only
  • Does not extend to general population

No Comprehensive SPA:

  • Texas has not submitted comprehensive State Plan Amendment for general CHW services coverage
  • MCO contracts may include CHW services as value-added benefits at plan discretion
  • No fee-for-service pathway for most CHW services

Significance: Texas demonstrates a paradox where mature workforce infrastructure coexists with limited financing sustainability. The 49 training programs produce certified CHWs without clear Medicaid pathway for most services.

SDOH/HRSN Status: FRAGMENTED / AT RISK

Factor Status
Screening MCO-specific, not mandated statewide
Navigation MCO-driven through individual plan contracts
Integration Model MCO-fragmented without state coordination
1115 Waiver Yes (Texas Healthcare Transformation) through 2030, no HRSN services

Key Limitation: CMS rescinded HRSN guidance in March 2025. Texas did not apply for HRSN services waiver under Biden-era framework. Current 1115 waiver focuses on managed care authority and maintaining uncompensated care pool rather than SDOH integration.

Survival Assessment: Social care components face high vulnerability given non-expansion status, lack of 1115 HRSN authority, and RHTP application emphasis on clinical infrastructure.

TD-E Classification: Partial (CHW certified but underfinanced, CIE fragmented)

GGH Strategic Assessment:

  • CIE Gap: MODERATE-HIGH (fragmented regional without statewide coordination)
  • Market Priority: MODERATE-HIGH
  • Entry Points: Texas Hospital Association, Texas Association of Community Health Centers, Texas DSHS CHW Program Office, Superior Health Plan, East Texas regional health systems
  • Target Geography: East Texas Region (Smith, Gregg, Harrison, Cherokee, Nacogdoches, Angelina, Rusk counties)
  • Offering: Platform + Services (Regional CIE deployment with CHW integration leveraging existing certified workforce, advocacy for comprehensive SPA)

CHW Program Status: EMERGING / SPA APPROVED

Factor Status
State Certification No (Uses C3 Core Competencies as framework)
Certifying Body None; C3 training accepted
Training Providers OK State Dept of Health, Tulsa City-County Health, OKC-County Health, OSU
Medicaid Reimbursement Yes (SPA Approved 2024)
Dedicated State Office Yes (NASHP 2024-2025 tracker confirms)

SPA Approval Details:

NASHP confirms Oklahoma among four states receiving CMS SPA approval in 2024 (alongside Colorado, Georgia, Washington):

  • Reimbursement pathway established for CHW services through Medicaid
  • Requirements: C3 training certificate OR 2,000 hours documented experience
  • Service setting: Must work at public health clinic; services ordered by physician/licensed practitioner
  • No statewide certification requirement

MCO Activity:

  • Oklahoma Complete Health offers CHW home visits for pediatric asthma management
  • Implemented as value-added service through managed care contract
  • Demonstrates MCO interest in CHW services

Tribal Health Workers:

  • Community Health Representatives (CHRs) operate through tribal programs
  • Distinct from state CHW certification/reimbursement framework
  • Long history of community-based care within tribal systems
  • Not integrated with state Medicaid SPA framework

Significance: Oklahoma’s 2024 SPA approval without certification requirement creates lower barrier to CHW service delivery while establishing Medicaid financing pathway.

SDOH/HRSN Status: LIMITED / AT RISK

Factor Status
Screening Not mandated statewide
Navigation Limited; managed care transition ongoing
Integration Model Emerging through managed care contracts
1115 Waiver Limited (managed care transition focus), no HRSN services

Survival Assessment: SDOH components face moderate-high vulnerability given non-expansion status, lack of 1115 HRSN authority, and ongoing managed care transition creating competing priorities. CHW services have established financing pathway through SPA.

TD-E Classification: Emerging (CHW financed, CIE minimal)

GGH Strategic Assessment:

  • CIE Gap: MODERATE (near-greenfield with Unite Oklahoma limited presence)
  • Market Priority: MODERATE
  • Entry Points: Oklahoma State Department of Health, Oklahoma Hospital Association, Oklahoma Primary Care Association, Oklahoma Complete Health (MCO with CHW interest), tribal health programs (distinct approach required)
  • Target Geography: Rural Eastern Oklahoma (Cherokee, Creek, Adair, Muskogee counties)
  • Offering: Platform + Services (CIE deployment combined with CHW services leveraging new SPA; tribal partnerships as separate track)
  • Challenges: Tribal CHR programs operate independently; managed care transition creates implementation competing priorities

California: CalAIM Saturation Closes Platform Market#

Research Date: January 29, 2026 Overall Status: FUNDED (CalAIM comprehensive, but certification paused, rural gaps) Confidence: High

RHTP Context

Metric Value
Year 1 Award $625,500,000
Rural Population ~4.63 million
Per Capita ~$135/rural resident
Lead Agency California Department of Health Care Services (DHCS)
Medicaid Status Expansion
CalAIM Status Active 1115 demonstration through 2026
Unique Complexity CalAIM supersedes traditional CIE model

California operates in a fundamentally different paradigm from Texas and Oklahoma. CalAIM (California Advancing and Innovating Medi-Cal) represents the nation’s most comprehensive Medicaid transformation, creating MCP-integrated social care delivery that supersedes traditional CIE platform approaches.

CIE Platform Status: CALAIM SUPERSEDES

Factor Status
Statewide Platform N/A (CalAIM/MCP-integrated)
Findhelp Presence Regional only; superseded by CalAIM
Unite Us Presence Regional only; superseded by CalAIM
Integration Model MCP-mandatory
RHTP Investment Supplements existing CalAIM infrastructure

CalAIM Framework:

Community Supports (14 Services):

  • Housing transition navigation services
  • Housing deposits
  • Housing tenancy and sustaining services
  • Short-term post-hospitalization housing
  • Recuperative care (medical respite)
  • Respite services
  • Day habilitation programs
  • Nursing facility transition/diversion to assisted living
  • Community transition services/nursing facility transition
  • Personal care and homemaker services
  • Environmental accessibility adaptations (home modifications)
  • Medically supportive food/nutrition services
  • Sobering centers
  • Asthma remediation

Enhanced Care Management (ECM):

  • Intensive care coordination for high-need populations
  • Populations of focus: experiencing homelessness, high utilizers, serious mental illness, pregnant/postpartum
  • Lead Care Manager model with comprehensive assessment
  • MCP-contracted providers deliver services

Data Exchange Framework (DxF):

  • State-mandated interoperability framework (effective January 2024)
  • Requires health and social service data exchange across organizations
  • Creates state-level coordination infrastructure

Implementation Reality:

CHCF 2024 Survey Findings:

  • Lower utilization than expected across ECM and Community Supports
  • ECM enrollment: 0.9% of eligible population
  • Community Supports enrollment: 0.9% of eligible population
  • MCP variation in eligibility criteria and implementation
  • Administrative burden cited as significant barrier

Rural Implementation Gaps:

  • Lower uptake in rural areas than urban
  • CBO capacity constraints limit Community Supports delivery
  • PATH Funding ($1.84 billion) targets rural and historically under-resourced areas
  • Standardization efforts underway (DHCS 2024-2025 action plan)

Significance: California’s CIE market is effectively closed for platform competition. MCPs serve as the integration mechanism; standalone CIE vendors must work through or alongside MCP infrastructure.

CHW Program Status: BENEFIT LIVE / CERTIFICATION PAUSED

Factor Status
State Certification Paused Indefinitely (November 2023)
Certifying Body HCAI (development paused)
Medi-Cal Benefit Yes (Effective July 2022)
Billing Codes CPT 98960-98962, Medicare G-codes (CHI/PIN)
Reimbursement Rate $27.54/30 min (2024) to $31.74/30 min (2025)

Medi-Cal CHW/P/R Benefit:

The Community Health Worker/Promotora/Representative (CHW/P/R) benefit became effective July 2022:

  • Reimbursable through CPT codes 98960-98962 and Medicare G-codes
  • Rate increase in 2025: $31.74 per 30 minutes (100% of Medicare RVUs)
  • Services must be under written recommendation from licensed provider
  • Plan of care required for services exceeding 6 hours

Supervising Provider Types:

  • Physicians, nurse practitioners, licensed clinical social workers
  • Pharmacists, mental health practitioners
  • CBOs and Local Health Jurisdictions (LHJs) added January 2024

Certification Pause:

November 2023: HCAI paused certification guidance following stakeholder concerns:

  • Feedback that stakeholders had not been adequately engaged
  • Concerns that proposed requirements not fit-for-purpose
  • Guidance paused indefinitely pending additional engagement

2024 Community Dialogues:

  • CalHHS, HCAI, and DHCS conducted second engagement round
  • 680+ CHW/P/Rs participated across 23 sessions hosted by 20 organizations
  • Re-examined workforce needs and certification approach
  • Advisory Workgroup established December 2024 (majority active CHW/P/Rs)

Sustainability Challenges:

Rate Insufficiency:

  • $31.74/30 min cited as insufficient for CBO sustainability
  • Administrative burden exceeds reimbursement value for many providers
  • CBOs struggle to maintain CHW programs on Medi-Cal reimbursement alone

Budget Cuts:

  • 2024 Budget Act eliminated most HCAI CHW/P/R funding
  • Limited state investment in workforce development
  • Certification development constrained by funding limitations

Significance: California paradox where benefit exists but sustainability mechanisms remain incomplete. Certification pause and inadequate rates limit workforce growth despite policy framework supporting CHW services.

SDOH/HRSN Status: FUNDED (CALAIM)

Factor Status
Screening MCP-mandated through CalAIM
Navigation ECM Lead Care Manager model
Integration Model MCP-integrated with Community Supports benefit
1115 Waiver Yes (CalAIM) through December 2026, 14 Community Supports services
Investment Level Billions through MCP contracts + IPP + PATH

Funding Mechanisms:

  • Incentive Payment Program (IPP): Performance payments to MCPs for ECM/Community Supports implementation
  • Provider Assistance for Transition to Health (PATH): $1.84 billion for providers serving rural/under-resourced areas
  • MCP Contract Requirements: MCPs required to offer ECM and Community Supports; investment built into rates

Survival Assessment: CalAIM represents most sustainable SDOH model nationally with waiver authority, MCP integration, and significant state investment. However, rural implementation gaps create service delivery opportunities distinct from platform opportunities.

TD-E Classification: Funded (CalAIM comprehensive, but certification paused, rural gaps)

GGH Strategic Assessment:

  • CIE Gap: LOW (CalAIM/MCP saturation)
  • Market Priority: LOW
  • Limited Opportunities: Rural Community Supports service delivery where CBO capacity insufficient; CHW services in rural areas with low ECM/Community Supports uptake
  • Not Recommended: Platform deployment (CalAIM/MCP infrastructure supersedes), statewide market entry (regulatory complexity), CHW workforce development (certification paused, funding eliminated)
  • If Pursued Entry Points: California Primary Care Association (rural FQHC needs), Central Valley MCPs (documented implementation challenges), PATH-funded providers seeking implementation support
  • Target Geography: If pursued, Central Valley or Northern California rural counties with lowest CalAIM uptake

Deep South Priority Region (Batch 7)#

Regional Overview#

The Deep South Priority states (MS, TN, GA, NC) present dramatically differentiated social care infrastructure maturity spanning from North Carolina’s nation-leading CIE deployment (NCCARE360 statewide) to Georgia’s complete absence of social care strategy in its RHTP application. The region divides into two distinct categories: states with established CIE infrastructure (TN, NC) showing mature platform deployments with MCO integration, and states without CIE infrastructure (MS, GA) demonstrating that non-expansion status correlates with social care deprioritization.

Key Regional Patterns:

  1. Non-expansion states deprioritize social care: Mississippi and Georgia RHTP applications emphasize clinical infrastructure over SDOH/CIE investment
  2. Binary CIE market structure: Tennessee and North Carolina have mature statewide platforms; Mississippi and Georgia have none
  3. CHW legislation creates sustainability pathway: Mississippi’s HB 1401 (2025) creates mandatory certification and directs Medicaid SPA development
  4. Platform maturity determines entry strategy: Mature platforms (TN, NC) require services approach; greenfield markets (MS, GA) require platform deployment
  5. Black Belt/Delta geography spans multiple states: Creating cross-state regional approach opportunity

Appalachian Priority Region (Batch 8)#

Regional Overview#

The Appalachian Priority states (OH, KY, WV) share robust CHW infrastructure without statewide CIE platforms despite having three expansion states with active care coordination programs. The region demonstrates that expansion status alone does not guarantee CIE development. All three states show preference for regional hub models over centralized systems, creating federated platform opportunities.

Key Regional Patterns:

  1. Expansion states lack statewide CIE: Ohio, Kentucky, and West Virginia all expanded Medicaid but have no statewide CIE coordination
  2. Robust CHW infrastructure without sustainable financing: MCO-mediated reimbursement limited to maternal/SUD populations
  3. Regional hub preference: Ohio Pathways HUBs, Kentucky Homeplace demonstrate regional rather than centralized approach
  4. Opioid crisis creates SUD-social care nexus: Providing reimbursement pathway for CHW services addressing substance use
  5. Gap is platform infrastructure, not workforce or program design
